Web & mobile design portfolio of Lee Munroe

Products I've designed & companies I've worked with

Mailgun by Rackspace

I joined Mailgun in 2013 to lead UX. I was their first full time design hire and I joined their small team of talented back-end engineers. Mailgun is an email API service for developers. After having gone through Y-Combinator the product was successfully acquired by Rackspace in 2012.

As a Product Designer my role involves looking after the full user experience, interface and front-end development, working closely with our team of engineers, operations and marketing. The bulk of the stack that I work with includes Python, Flask and SASS.

Visit Mailgun

Flask.io

Create really simple and sharable to do lists with Flask. No sign up. Nothing to download. Just add tasks. A simple productivity tool for getting things done and sharing lists with friends. Featured in LifeHacker here.

Flask is a side project that I designed and developed myself using Ruby on Rails. It started off as a weekend experiment to learn CoffeeScript. I now use it as a playground to experiment with new ideas, tools and keep my development skills up to date. It has 1,000 active monthly users.

Visit Flask.io

CodeShare.io

Share code real time with other developers. In 2013 I hacked together this free code sharing tool with Firebase, using Node.js and Express. The purpose of this project was to learn Node.js, which we were using at the time for Runnable, and experiment with the Firebase API.

Copy/paste and share real-time code snippets with others. If you share the url with a friend they will see your code as you type it. CodeShare has 3,000 active monthly users.

Visit CodeShare

Runnable

In 2012 I joined Runnable, based in Palo Alto, becoming a member of the founding 5-man team and sole Designer. Runnable enables developers to run server-side code in the browser. No downloading, nothing to install, no registration, just run code. Runnable has raised $2m to date.

Daily activities involved product discovery, prototyping, user studies, interface design, HTML/CSS markup, integration with Node.js and Javascript and working closely with the engineers and founder.

As an early stage lean startup, I spent a lot of time running experiments, talking to users and using tools like Mixpanel, Optimizely and Qualaroo to help validate assumptions and determine the product development roadmap, while tackling any design related tasks that needed done, e.g. designing our logo.

Visit Runnable

Kareo

Based in Irvine, Orange County CA, Kareo develop EHR and Practice Management software. I joined as their first Design hire, then led the UX Team from 2011-2012 helping them transition from a desktop to web application, and introducing their first mobile app. The company grew from 40 to 200 people during that time.

Daily activities involved product discovery, talking to customers, lots of prototyping, running usability tests, designing and marking up the UI, working closely with developers and product managers and managing the UX team.

Visit Kareo

Twit Amore

Twit Amore was a project of mine that went live on Valentine's Day 2012. It's a quirky website that tells you who you love on Twitter. I used it as an experiment to learn some Ruby on Rails, along with Haml, Sass, Twitter API and was my first project using Heroku.

Due to the tie in with Valentine's Day the site went viral and managed to get on the front page of Mashable, The Next Web and Huffington Post among others. The site received over 1 million unique visitors in 2012.

Visit Twit Amore

Lookaly

The best way to describe Lookaly is Yelp for Northern Ireland.

As Creative Director and Co-Founder I designed the user experience, interface, branding, HTML/CSS and took care of product, marketing and sales.

The site is well received in Northern Ireland attracting over 40 thousand unique visitors per month.

Visit Lookaly

The Big Word Project

Back in University I teamed up with classmate Paddy Donnelly and created a website that let people buy words of the dictionary, priced at $1 per letter, and link them to their website.

It was a viral success. We managed to sell over 7,500 words and it led to a feature article in Wired magazine.

Visit TBWP
